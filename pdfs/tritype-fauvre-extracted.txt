TRITYPE THEORY OVERVIEW
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Every person has a tritype — one type from each of the three Centers of Intelligence:
  Body Center (types 8, 9, 1) — Instinctual, gut-based, anger-driven
  Heart Center (types 2, 3, 4) — Relational, image-based, shame-driven
  Head Center (types 5, 6, 7) — Cognitive, fear-based, planning-driven

The tritype is written as three digits in dominant order. The first digit is the
primary type (most energy, most automatic), the second is the type in the next
most activated center, the third is the type in the least activated center.

Example: 469 means Heart-dominant (4), Head-support (6), Body-support (9).
The core strategy is finding deep meaning and authenticity (4), supported by
scanning for threat and seeking certainty (6), and avoiding conflict (9).

TRITYPE SHAPES EXPRESSION:
Two people can share the same core type but have very different tritypes, making
them feel like completely different personalities. A Type 4 with tritype 468 will
feel more intense, confrontational, and powerful than a Type 4 with tritype 479,
who will feel more optimistic, dreamy, and conflict-avoidant.

DOMINANT ORDER MATTERS:
The dominant type in each center is not always the highest-numbered or
stereotypical expression of that center. Within the Body Center, for example,
a person might lead with Type 9 (peaceful, avoiding) rather than Type 8
(confrontational, powerful). The dominant type is the one they default to under
stress or when seeking safety.

TRITYPE CORE TRIGGERS AS ASSESSMENT QUESTION SEEDS
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Core triggers are the situations that reliably activate the tritype's reactive
pattern. They are excellent question seeds because they surface the person's
automatic response before they can edit it.

BETRAYAL triggers (846, 826, 836, 946): Ask about trust, promises broken,
  people who turned out to be different from what they seemed.
INJUSTICE triggers (812, 813, 826, 836, 846): Ask about situations where
  someone was treated unfairly and what they did about it.
INCOMPETENCE triggers (813, 835, 825, 125, 135): Ask about working with
  people who aren't as capable as expected.
CONFLICT triggers (912, 913, 926, 936, 945, 946): Ask about their first
  instinct when a disagreement is brewing.
RESTRICTION/LIMITATION triggers (147, 847, 947, 137, 379): Ask about what
  happens inside them when they're told something is impossible or not allowed.
INAUTHENTICITY triggers (145, 146, 147, 846, 814, 468): Ask about situations
  where they had to be someone they're not.
ABANDONMENT triggers (126, 946, 269): Ask about their relationship to the
  fear that important people will leave.
INGRATITUDE triggers (125, 127, 912): Ask what happens when they give a lot
  and it goes unacknowledged.

TWO TYPE 4s WITH DIFFERENT TRITYPES:
4-6-8 (The Truth Teller): Intense, confrontational, shame-and-rage reactive pattern.
  Tests people. Pushes back. Defensive and probing at once.
4-7-9 (The Gentle Visionary): Dreamy, optimistic, avoids pain through beauty.
  Accommodating. Conflict-avoidant. Much softer outer presentation.

TWO TYPE 6s WITH DIFFERENT TRITYPES:
6-2-9 (The Good Samaritan): Warm, self-erasing, anxious about abandonment.
  Devoted. Merges with loved ones. Rarely confrontational.

  Counter-phobic. Tests everyone. Harder outer shell.

TWO TYPE 9s WITH DIFFERENT TRITYPES:
9-2-6 (The Good Samaritan): Gives endlessly, anxious about harmony.
  Service-oriented. Hard to separate self from others.
9-4-5 (The Contemplative): Withdrawn, introspective, inwardly rich.
  Needs solitude. Less relationally accommodating.

TWO TYPE 8s WITH DIFFERENT TRITYPES:
8-1-4 (The Fierce Visionary): Idealistic, creative, has a strong moral code.
  Passionate. Gets attached to being right.
8-6-2 (The Defender): Community-protective, loyal, gives fiercely to the tribe.
  Tests trust. Deeply relationship-oriented despite the armor.

INSTINCTUAL STACKING INFLUENCE ON TRITYPE EXPRESSION
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

The instinctual stacking (SP/SO/SX) modifies how the tritype expresses itself:

SP-FIRST + any tritype: Makes the tritype expression more private, resourceful,
and materially grounded. The person tends to work things out internally before
expressing them. May appear more controlled and less emotionally readable.

SO-FIRST + any tritype: Makes the tritype expression more relational, purposeful,
and group-oriented. The person tends to express the tritype through their role
in communities, work, or social causes.

SX-FIRST + any tritype: Makes the tritype expression more intense, merged,
and one-to-one. The person tends to express the tritype through deep bonds
with specific people. May appear more dramatic and emotionally overwhelming.

EXAMPLE: Tritype 469 with SP-first looks like a quietly anxious, introspective
person who processes longing internally and rarely shows it. The same tritype
with SX-first looks like someone consumed by longing for a specific person,
intensely reactive to any sign of abandonment.

Enneastyle Presentation
The Enneagram and The World of Image and Self-Projection
The 9 Languages of Enneagram Type
Author’s Note
In 1969, I began a career in the field of image, presentation and style, with a focus on personal empowerment.
Later, as a corporate manager and then as an image consultant, I learned a great deal about how people see
themselves and others. In particular, I learned that being in alignment with one’s true self is what supports
individuals in projecting that which most accurately expresses who they are and what they feel.
In the 1980s, the ‘Baby Boomers’ came of age and entered the work force. To move into management
and executive positions in such a large and varied group, it became essential to stand out. Self help books
and management training classes became readily available to help ‘boomers’ succeed. Learning to ‘dress
for success’ was part of this trend. I was introduced to this concept and others and began teaching these
principles in the corporate world. It was at this time, that I first recognized and began to theorize about the
difference between how people perceive themselves and how they are perceived by others.
In 1985, I discovered the Enneagram, a nine point personality typology which recognizes these differences
as nine distinct survival strategies. This discovery helped shed light on the incongruence between one’s
internal perceptions and the perceptions of others, highlighting an area often obscured from consciousness,
while suggesting possible reasons and underlying motivations for the disparity.
Overlaying the different patterns relating to image with the Enneagram, I found what appeared to be
correlations between the nine Enneagram points and what they wished to project. As a result, I embarked
on a journey of discovery to experiment with the theory. I took a characteristically playful approach, while
simultaneously searching for a deeper, more personal look at the motivations of image, presentation and
style. This research took the form of observations, questionnaires and personal interviews. The initial
findings of Enneastyle were so powerful that I continued my research and began to explore the influence of
the Enneagram Instinctual Subtypes as well. Further inspired by my findings with the Instinctual Subtypes
I have continued to research many areas related to the Enneagram.
What first piqued my interest in doing this study was the fact that people in the Enneagram community
would decide that an individual was or wasn’t a certain type based on the appearance of that individual.
Since this was usually based on a first impression, I felt that the visual appearance was a strong indicator
in formulating an idea of someone’s potential Enneagram Type.

Later, when those in the community became more familiar with an individual, they came to realize that
their first impression may not have been accurate. The key factor in adjusting their first impression was
hearing the individual discuss their experiences on a panel with those of the same Enneagram Type. It was
the personal sharing that enabled the observer to more fully see the individual as their type.
As most Enneagram enthusiasts have discovered, personal sharing of one’s internal experience is a more
important indicator of one’s potential type than the visual first impression. However, the first impression
did hold significant keys to understanding the similarities and differences within type.
Research has revealed that evaluating one’s appearance-visual look, archetype, manner and presentation
is 80% accurate in determining someone’s Enneagram Type as there are archetypal themes for each
type. With experience, one can begin to see patterns within type and a trained eye can further see micro
expressions and how tension is carried in the face and body. What is critical, however, is to be able to
accurately type the other 20% that do not match the archetype of their Enneagram Type. Intrigued by the
20%, I wished to learn more.
When I first worked with the Enneagram, there were no visual representations of the 9 Enneagram Types
or the Instinctual Subtypes. At first glance, fellow Enneagram enthusiasts might think that I was a 7, 3, 4
or 2. I imagined that this was due to my experience in an image related field. And, since this assumption
was made prior to any discussion with me, I realized that this perception must be largely based on my
outward presentation-in other words, I must project a visual image that to others may not initially appear
to be type 8. In an effort to understand the significance of these perceptions, I began to study the manner
in which the Enneagram Types dressed and why.
As I explored these distinctions, I discovered that I did not see myself, nor would I choose to describe
myself, in the same language that others might. I also found that some people would describe me using the
same words that they would use to describe themselves or others. I began to wonder whether people of the
same type shared a common vocabulary. For example, I noticed that Sevens might say, “You look fun!”
whereas Nines usually said, “You look nice” or “You look comfortable.” Carrying this further, I wondered
if each Enneagram Type had its own image and lexicon, and if so, whether or not it matched the type’s
internal experience of themselves and image.
Illustrations
The question is, do each of the nine Enneagram Types describe themselves in the same manner as others
of their type, and if so, do they visually project a similar image?
To begin to see the difference between how you see yourself and how you are seen by others, think of
five words you would use to describe yourself. Then ask a partner to select five words they would use to
describe you, in particular asking the partner to recall his or her first impression of you. Finally, compare
the words you used to describe yourself with the words your partner chose to describe you.

In most cases, the words chosen by each person were probably somewhat different, although they may

The Enneagram and Image
A person’s Enneagram Type and and a person’s image share much in common. The Enneagram type
identifies the internal experience of an individual whereas the image is the external expression of the
person’s image. Incorporating the Enneagram with issues of personal presentation is a useful way to
determine the underlying motivations of an individual, thereby clarifying the distinctions between one’s
internal experience and its outward expression.
As with any business, in the world of image, it is imperative that the consultant interview and inquire
in-depth as to the needs, concerns, assets and liabilities of the client. This is necessary in order to build
rapport, ascertain needs, establish priorities and ultimately meet the client’s expectations. The same is true
in the Enneagram world, as it is through the interviewing process that the deeper motivations and more
hidden aspects of personality are revealed. It is the deeper motivation beneath the Enneagram type that
defines the desires and fears of the individual, illuminating both the gifts and the obstacles of each type.
This underlying motivation is often unconscious, yet influences every aspect of the personality, including
one’s image.
Image consultants address these aspects by recognizing that one’s image is based upon the totality of an
individual. As a result, consultants quickly learn that for one to have a true sense of personal power and
presence, it is imperative that one’s external image match their internal experience and personality. This is
what creates a sense of harmony.
Just as in any facet of life, the whole of one’s personal presentation is far greater than the sum of its parts.
For it is the integration of all aspects of the personality: body type, coloring, instincts, spiritual focus,
energy, image, personal gifts and struggles that create the harmonic, balanced and complete whole. Just
as we appreciate beauty in nature, we appreciate it when someone dresses and behaves in a manner that is
congruent with their energy, archetype, passions, expressions, personality, features and body type.
Research has revealed that it is innately human to feel awe and at ease when we are in the presence of
beauty. We feel comfortable with others who are appealing, but the most comfortable with those that are

or pattern that unites and combines different colors, textures, themes and styles, thus creating a sense of
relatedness and harmony. In the human condition, each individual is a combination of diverse elements
which includes one’s appearance, manner and personality. Perhaps the connecting piece is the soul.
Method of Study
To begin this study, I prepared the Enneastyle Questionnaire, which was designed to elicit information
as to how people already familiar with the Enneagram perceived their own visual image. Initially, I sent
the questionnaire to 400-plus Enneagram enthusiasts. While the responses were revealing, I wondered if
the answers to the first questionnaire were influenced by the fact that the participants already knew their
Enneagram Type. So I began a blind study of people that did not know their Enneagram Type to compare
the word usage of the non-Enneagram savvy.
I used the initial information, along with the follow up data obtained during personal interviews, to
prepare charts and diagrams to illustrate the initial results. This portion of the study involved only the first
objective-- to demonstrate that there is a strong correlation between the Enneagram point motivations and
the visual images they wish to project. To date, I have received approximately 17 thousand responses.
The second part of this study involves the Enneastyle Questionnaire #2, which was intended to elicit
information concerning the second objective-- to demonstrate that there is a notable difference between
the visual images that each Enneagram Type wishes to project and the visual images that are actually
received by the other eight types. This second objective arose as a result of the information obtained from
the first part of the study-- that, in fact, there is a contradiction between how each Enneagram point sees
itself and how it is perceived by others. For example, Enneagram Type Fours saw themselves as tasteful,
elegant and aesthetic, whereas Fours were often perceived by others as flamboyant, theatrical and overly
dramatic. 4s never used the word flamboyant to describe themselves and found the word to be tasteless.
A third step in the research was to ask participants to send images that reflected an aspect of their Enneagram
Type or their Enneagram Instinctual Subtype. These visual images were usually magazine clippings of
landscapes, faces, ads, words, phrases or artwork. I used these images, along with images of my own, as
the basis for collages of each of the nine Enneagram Types.
I then invited Enneagram enthusiasts of each Enneagram Type to review the collage of their respective
type. I asked them to confirm that the collage accurately depicted their Enneagram Type concerns. I then
noted any comments they made about images on their own collage. I also showed them the other eight
collages and noted any comments they made about those as well.

Patterns in the Data
There was a surprising degree of consistency not only in the kinds of images chosen, but in the responses
to those images by others of the same Type.
For example, of the initial responses from the Enneagram Type Threes, I received multiple images that
were very similar in energy and expression, depicting people in the pose of victory. Another example,
was that all of the Sevens sent bright, colorful images, and that over half of these images depicted people
celebrating in some manner.
Eights also liked intense and colorful images. Eights and Nines seemed to prefer larger images. In fact, in
the case of the Nines, there were very few small images. When interviewed, the Nines stated that they felt
most comfortable with the open expanse of the larger images.
Both the Fours and Fives chose images that were deeper in color and more complex. The Fours, however,
tended to have multiple shades of red. When interviewed, they defined this element as an identification
with red as being the color of passion and intensity.
Twos chose the most diverse images, which at first seem to create less of a pattern than the other eight
collages. When interviewed, the Twos explained that the inconsistency of images related to the need to be
able to alter and match their circumstances to feel safe and at ease.
Sixes had images that directly contradicted one another, depicting both the phobic and counter-phobic
expression of the type. The Sixes also had the most group images. When interviewed, the Sixes reported
that they primarily identified with either the phobic images or the counter-phobic images. Then, depending
on the situation, they felt that they could alternate between the two extremes.
The Ones had the most structured, ‘straight-laced’ images, contrasted with images of cherubs, hearts and
angels, reminiscent of the Victorian era. When interviewed, the Ones stated that they felt that it took great
effort to work so hard at being good. They felt that they had to have high personal standards to stay above
criticism, as everyone had a different idea of what was considered good.
Another area of consistency was the reaction of some types to certain images on the collage of their
Enneagram Type. For example, most of the Fours reacted strongly to the image of a little brown monkey
on the Four collage. Generally, the initial comment from Fours was that they would have chosen a monkey
that was more unique or rare such as a monkey that had long, beautiful white fur. When I asked if I should
remove the image from the Four collage the Fours decided that I should leave it. The 4s explained that
the truth was they were a sad little brown monkey that wanted to be a beautiful white monkey. This selfawareness is one of the gifts of Fours--the ability to recognize who they are and that they want to be
special due to a deep fear of being ordinary.

Enneagram General
Enneagram Background
The Enneagram is a dynamic personality system that combines sophisticated modern psychological tools
with ancient wisdom. The Enneagram (any•a•gram; ennea is Greek for nine, and gram means drawing)
is a circle enclosing nine equidistant points connected by nine intersecting lines. This unusual symbol
has its roots in the Middle East in ancient spiritual traditions, and research suggests it dates as far back
as Pythagoras. The nine points represent the ways in which the nine different personality types perceive
and defend their realities. As we know it today, the Enneagram is a profound and vital link between
Eastern spirituality and Western psychology. It is a conceptual system, a theory of personality types that
is complex and sophisticated and yet is a sensible and easily understood tool for self-discovery.
The wisdom of the Enneagram is that it recognizes nine very different, yet inherently valid views of reality.
The power of the Enneagram is that it is a profound and comprehensive tool to harness and transform selfdefeating behavior into life-enhancing personal empowerment. The gift of the Enneagram is that through
self-discovery, one can create and sustain meaningful and lasting relationships.
The Enneagram is a useful tool in determining personal patterns of relating and in understanding
differing styles of communicating. Through deeper insight, one can more fully understand his or her
own communication style, as well as the styles of others. With this knowledge, one can observe the core
triggers that influence one’s ability to successfully respond to any given situation and avoid ineffectual,
self defeating reactivity. Ultimately, the Enneagram is an invaluable tool that defines nine distinct life
strategies which, when understood, can give crucial insight into developing critical self awareness.
Wings
Each Enneagram Type is flanked on either side by two other types. These other two types are known as
‘wing’ types. The Enneagram Type is a product of the tension between the issues of these two Enneagram
types. Both wings are always present, however, one of the wing types may be more expressed and
influential. For example, all Fives are focused on gathering information to be safe and secure in the world.
A Five with a Four wing may be more romantic and creative than a Five with a Six wing who may be more
scientific and focused on issues of loyalty.
Stress and Heart or Security Points
Each Enneagram Type is connected by a line to two other types. These connections are known as stress
and security, or heart points. When the primary Enneagram Type is ineffective in dealing with a difficult
situation, it will employ the strategies of the two types along the lines of connection. This is known as the
inner flow within the Enneagram symbol.

Enneagram Centers of Intelligence
There are nine Enneagram Types that are grouped into three triads. There are three Enneagram Types in
each triad. Each triad represents a different center of intelligence. In human nature, we use all three centers
but tend to over use one of these three centers. In the center that we overuse, there is an Enneagram Type
that is dominant. This is one’s Enneagram Type.
The instinctual or gut center (8-9-1) is body-based and can be seen as having a desire to take action in the
world. They are gutsy, instinctive and visceral. Their primary issues are judgment, denial and resistance.
They struggle with being asleep to their true self. Whether or not they overtly express it, they are motivated
and take action when they are angry. Fearing neglect or being overlooked, they seek acceptance. As
children, they often felt they lacked the space to be or exist. They want to be respected for who they are.
The emotional or heart center (2-3-4) is feeling-based, and can be seen as having a desire to focus on the
needs of others and to positively affect others in the world. They are heartfelt, emotive and adaptable. Their 
Enneastyle • Katherine Fauvre Enneagram Consulting • www.katherinefauvre.com• © 1995-2017 Katherine Chernick Fauvre • rev. 2017 10
primary issues are of craving attention from and wanting to be attached to others. They may struggle with
being identified with their image or ‘false’ self. Whether or not they overtly express it, they are motivated
by and take action when they have strong feelings. Fearing grief, shame or being ignored, they seek
attention and affirmation. As children, they often felt they lacked accurate mirroring. They want to be
seen and valued by others.
The mental or thinking center (5-6-7) is thought-based and can be seen as having a desire to give greater
importance to the interior world of ideas. They are knowledgeable, intellectual and visionary. Their
primary issues are aversion, and projection with a focus on authority. They struggle with preconceptions
and the fear of separation from others. Whether or not they overtly express it, they are motivated by and
take action when they feel fear and anxiety. Fearing chaos and their inability to cope with it, they seek
reassurance, whether in a theology, a philosophy or a kind word. As children, they often felt they lacked
trustworthy and consistent guidance. They want to be clear-minded and certain.
Instinctual Subtypes and Stacking
There are three instinctual drives that govern the manner in which one will tend to issues of survival.
There are nine Enneagram Types and three Instincts resulting in 27 Instinctual Subtypes. In the context of
the Enneagram, these three drives, or ‘Instincts,’ are referred to as Self-Preservation, Social and Sexual
respectively.
Whether consciously expressed or not, we exhibit all three of these instinctual drives in varying degrees
throughout the day. However, generally there is one instinct that is more dominant and influential in our
lives. This is called one’s dominant Instinctual Subtype.
Each Enneagram Type is flavored by the influence of one of these instincts. Different stages in life and
extreme stress will bring these drives into focus at different times, but the method of solution is governed
by one’s dominant instinctual drive. For example, we all tend to focus on our health when we are ill (sp),
our social standing when we feel excluded (so), and our attractiveness when we feel undesirable (sx).

Image Concepts
Personal Image and Self-Projection
For those unfamiliar with the concept of personal image, a few facts may prove of assistance:
• Image comprises the way we walk, our carriage, our energy, our speech, our tone, our language
choices and our dress, resulting in our overall visual and energetic presentation.
• Appearance is our non-verbal communication to the world.
• A first impression is made in 20 seconds. Recent research suggests that it may be made in as few
as 2-3 seconds. The rest of the time is spent verifying the initial experience.
• You only have one chance to make a first impression, which, once made, rarely if ever changes.
And, the first impression continues to come to mind even when it has been adjusted to include
additional factors.
• All of us have an image, whether consciously orchestrated and developed, or unconsciously ignored
and neglected.
• “No image” is a powerful image statement— one that is often misconstrued by the observer—
for it is the observer’s standards, experiences and associations that determine if the supposed ‘no
image’ is viewed as positive or negative.
• We can consciously or unconsciously judge ourselves and others by appearances.
• Our interests, talents, preoccupations and motivations are all influential in creating our overall
image or visual presentation.
• Remember, we need to communicate and we need to dress, but how and in what manner we elect
to do so is a conscious choice. In other words, an artfully planned presentation is one choice, just
as an unaware, unplanned presentation is equally a choice— ironically, both are reflections of how
we feel or wish to be perceived.
• Therefore, our image is a reflection of where our attention goes, how we feel about ourselves, and
how we choose to express both.

Enneastyle Findings
Preliminary Findings:
Influence of Enneagram Type in Vocabulary
In general, the choices of words or adjectives did vary from person to person, for image, like any field, has
its own language and vocabulary. However, even when the words chosen were different, they still tended
to have similar meaning and energy. I also found that there was a consistent vocabulary shared by each
Enneagram Type.
There were similar words used by all the types, as we share the same generic language and vocabulary.
Even when English was a second language participants selected the same adjectives as those that shared
the same Enneagram Type. The differences between how the nine Enneagram Types describe themselves
can often be subtle yet ultimately proved to be distinct and meaningful.
6 versus 4
At first glance, some of the words used by Fours and Sixes appear to be similar. The distinguishing factor,
however, was their far greater differences in the additional words selected. For example, both Fours and
Sixes used adjectives such as sensitive, warm, friendly, kind and caring.
However, Sixes also chose words such as contemporary, related, cautious, loyal, nice, anxious, wary,
reliable, imaginative, dedicated and faithful (as well as fearless, on the edge and nonconformist for the
counter-phobic Six), whereas Fours chose additional words like aesthetic, tasteful, elite, dramatic, special,
intense, elegant, creative, beautiful, expressive, mysterious, graceful and intuitive.
These differences identify the Six’s need to match their peers and avoid being a target of criticism, and the
4 need to be individualistic, original, special and unique. Underneath these needs is a corresponding fear.
Both Four and Six fear abandonment so they can be common look-a-likes. However, the Six fears being
alone, unsafe and unprotected whereas the 4 fears being inadequate, flawed and defective. So, one needs
to identify someone’s deeper concerns to accurately determine Enneagram type.
3 versus 8
Threes and Eights are both dynamic types. They both chose the adjectives dynamic, confident, competent,
creative, impressive and positive. The Three further described themselves as polished, highly capable,
efficient, slick, singular and outstanding whereas the Eight described themselves as authentic, opinionated,
straightforward, natural, striking, commanding and distinctive.
When interviewed, the Threes stated that they needed to match the fashion but more importantly, the
expectations

of others whereas the Eights said that they followed their own muse and dressed for themselves and may
or may not be in fashion. In fact, the Three further said that they would never use the word opinionated
as they felt it could be seen as potentially negative, and the Eights said they would never use the word
capable as they already saw themselves as capable.
In addition to word choices and explanations, the body language is very different with each type. Using
the earlier example, Threes and Eights have very different body language from one another as well as
different reasons for their clothing choices. For example, the Eight walks with long strides and a swagger
and does not feel natural in tight fitting clothes that require poise. They like things that stretch and
are comfortable. The Eight also stated that they needed clothing that moves and matches their energy.
Conversely, the Three walks according to what they are wearing and the image they wish to project. So,
they felt comfortable in clothing that requires smaller strides if the occasion called for a particular look,
especially if the look would give them a winning image.
This example highlights key distinguishing factors between the Three and the Eight. Most importantly,
it highlights the 8 need to be comfortable and at ease, along with ‘what you see is what you get,’ and the
Three need to be polished and make a good first impression. Underneath these clothing concerns are two
very different motivations and two very different concerns about image. The Three wants to have finesse
and be seen as successful, whereas the Eight wants to have power and be seen as credible. Ultimately, the
8 wants to be successful but the need for power leads, and the Three wants to be powerful but ultimately
the need to be successful leads.
Influence of Wings
Often, the image was a combination of the type and the preferred or dominant wing. For example, a Two
with a One wing might wish to look appropriately attractive, a Five with a Four Wing may want to appear
effortlessly special, and an Eight with a Seven wing might prefer plenty of variety. In addition, an Eight
with a Seven wing tends to dress in brighter colors and speak at a faster tempo than an Eight with a Nine
wing, who prefers more simplicity and speaks at a slower tempo. Many of the types reported more of an
affinity with one of their wing types, and tended to lean strongly in that direction especially when selecting
clothing. Often this is observable and can be seen in one’s manner, dress and/or behavior. However,
some types reported that they equally experience aspects of both wings.
This would appear to suggest or support a “pure type.” Ironically, this neutral expression of type is not
necessarily observable. When questioned further, these types were able to differentiate the influence
of their wings in two ways—both internally and externally. Therefore, it is important to note that there
are some aspects of image that are observable to others, as well as equally strong influences that are unmanifested visually, and thus, unobservable to others. These manifested and un-manifested aspects are
equally important to one’s overall image.
Further research indicates that both wings are always present and equal in strength for one’s Type is a
result of the tension between the two wings. Although many people may identify more strongly with one
wing than the other, in my interviews, I found that both wings are expressed equally. For example, one

client, a Social Nine, tended to dress conservatively and seek perfection, in accordance with her One wing,
but felt a strong sense of justice that would compel her to take action when her 8 wing emerged. While the
1 wing was readily visible from the outside, it wasn’t until she worked in an in-depth one on one coaching
session that the strength of her 8 wing became evident.
Influence of Stress and Heart Points
Sometimes the stress and heart types were expressed in choices. For example, a Nine in Six “doubt”
responded with, “I don’t know if I answered the question correctly, I just want to look competent (perhaps
a move to Three?),” whereas a CP Six (sexual subtype) dressed for attractiveness and security, choosing
to combine the elements of success (3) and comfort (9).
There were some types who chose to answer two different ways. A good illustration was a 1 with a 2
wing (sexual subtype) who simply filled out the questionnaire twice—once with the appropriate “correct”
answers (1), with a desire for acceptance, and a second time with her more “imperfect” but heartfelt
reality, with a desire to be her more playful (7) or unique (4) self.
Visual Archetypes
There were also some findings regarding the ‘look’ of each Enneagram type. When possible, I compared
the seven basic archetypes: priest/priestess, server, artisan, sage, king/queen, warrior and scholar to each
Enneagram type. As one might imagine, all seven archetypes were seen in all nine Enneagram types and
in each of the instinctual subtypes. However, there did seem to be some archetypes that stood out with
respect to each type and instinctual subtype. These matched the stereotypes for each Enneagram type.
For example, the most common stereotype for the 8 is the warrior archetype, for the 1 is the king/queen
archetype, for the 4 is the artisan and for the 5 is the scholar.
Any type can be a warrior but at first glance one will generally think that the warrior is the 8. So when a 5
has the warrior archetype rather than the scholar it can be more difficult to accurately type this individual.
The warrior 5 is extremely rare and is a good example of the 20% that are atypical examples. What I did
find is that the warrior 5 usually has the self-preserving instinct dominant. The same is true for the warrior
4 and 7.
Another example, is the 6 with the king/queen archetype. At first glace they appear to be 8s as well. They
are far more comfortable leading than the 6 with the scholar or server archetype. They will, however, still
privately worry and focus on planning strategies to manage their fears and worst case scenarios whereas
the king/queen 8 will not, as fear is not a part of the 8s defense strategy.
Accurately typing the 20% that are the exceptions to the rule is more complex. The most relevant
distinguishing features with look-a-likes emerged when I compared the language use and/or micro
expressions of each type.
For example, the 8 is not evasive and does not regularly use the phrase ‘it depends.’ like the 6. Nor does
the 8 regularly cock their head in confusion or look around scanning. They instead will have a steely gaze

that is often intimidating to others and speak in imperatives. The 6 has the social role of monitoring and
safe guarding, so, it stands to reason that they would have micro expressions that demonstrate concern.
The 8 has the social role of taking charge, leading and protecting, so, it makes sense that they would exude
confidence, have a strong jaw and speak in a commanding, nonchalant manner.
Influence of Instinctual Subtypes
When I sorted the questionnaires by instinctual subtype, I was struck by the common language used by
each instinct. In fact, the similarities shared by the three instincts were even greater than the language
shared by the nine Enneagram Types. For example, all types with the same instinct had more in common
with one another than they did with those that shared the same Enneagram type. This finding inspired my
subsequent research, The Study of the Instinctual Subtypes.
This was especially noticeable in the one-to-one, or sexual, subtype, whose questionnaires had one or more
adjectives describing a desire to be alluring, beautiful, handsome and attractive, and dress accordingly.
For example, one sexual 5 with a 4 wing described herself as alluring but reserved, with a desire to look
beautiful. The sexual subtypes were often more intense, fluid and provocative, with high energy projected
through the eyes. The sexual subtypes often carried the artisan and priest/priestess archetypes.
The social subtype repeatedly expressed a desire to fit in (with their desired group or peers), to dress for
the occasion and the need to have feedback. For example, a social 3 with both wings described herself
as varied, creative but controlled, and looking like she fit in while still standing apart and singular, and
dressing for the occasion.
The archetypes of the social subtypes were either more intellectual, focusing on
cultural ideas, or more robust focusing on affiliations with head energy split between the individual and
the group or others. The introverted intellectual social subtype carried the scholar archetype, whereas the
extraverted robust social subtype carried the hero archetype.
The self-preservation subtype seemed to focus on being traditional, practical, conservative and most of all
comfortable. This was true at any age. This need for comfort was further amplified if the self-preservation
subtype was a also a ‘gut type’. For example, a self-preservation 8 with a 9 wing described herself as
casual, authentic and dressing for comfort, with her overall look as functional, understated and avoiding
pretense. The archetypes of the self-preservation subtypes were often solid and steadfast, with server and
warrior types.
These instinctual subtype findings were powerful, however, the Enneagram type was still influential,
especially in the words chosen by each type. For example, a self-preservation 1 wanted to be appropriate,
conservative and comfortable, whereas a self-preservation 4 wanted to be unique, statuesque, fastidious and
stylishly comfortable, and a self-preservation 7 wanted to be subtle but noticed, practical and comfortable.
The archetype of the self-preservation subtypes were more steadfast and grounded, and their energy is
carried in the belly. Therefore, as an observer, we will know that those that dress in an unusual manner are

When I interviewed study participants it became clear that one’s instinctual types are ‘stacked’ in their

Influence of Centers
Even in a shared center or triad, there were variations on theme. The general desire and vocabulary
of the centers were similar, but what was intriguing was that often the wings represented the two sides
of the receptive center. For example, in the gut center, the 9, who simply wants to be unassuming and
comfortable, is flanked on one side by the 8 who wants to be impressively authentic and on the other side
by the 1 who wants to be appropriately correct.
As another example, the desire of the heart center is being noticed, yet the focus of attention by type was
different and was acted out in three different ways. For example, the focus of attention for the 2 was on
being appealing, the focus of attention for the 3 was appearing competent, and the focus of attention for
the 4 was on being special and unique.
 In addition, sexual subtypes that are heart types looked like the stereotype of the sexual 2, 3 or 4. However,
sexual subtypes that were head or gut types also looked like heart types. This seems to confirm that there
is a similarity in appearance between the three instinctual subtypes and the three centers of intelligence.
Concluding Thoughts
I was deeply touched and intimately informed by the candor, confusion, humor, and self-verification
of image issues contained in the responses. The responses to the questionnaire and interviews were as
sensitive and diverse as we all are as individuals. However, just as the Enneagram types so aptly define
our inner world of defense and motivation, it appears that the same was true of image and style.
A noteworthy aspect of this research was the way in which the questionnaire was approached. Some
were skeptical, some diligently answered all the questions, others played with their responses with wit
and humor, still others needed clarification and made suggestions for improvement, and there were those
who offered help and support. Does this not in fact support our Enneagram type and where our attention
goes? Does this not perhaps demonstrate the possible focus of our fears and desires about one of the most
personal aspects of how we see and ultimately express ourselves? I would suggest that it may.
The true gift, however, was the intimate self-verification of how each Enneagram Type views the issue
of image. For each type, there began to emerge a central theme that corresponded with the type’s core
motivation: the Ones need of appropriate attire, the Twos need to be appealing, the Threes need to be 
Enneastyle • Katherine Fauvre Enneagram Consulting • www.katherinefauvre.com• © 1995-2017 Katherine Chernick Fauvre • rev. 2017 18
polished, the Fours need to be special, the Fives need of camouflage, the Sixes need to be secure, the
Sevens search for a touch of flair, the Eights no-nonsense approach to image as a statement of “I dress
for myself”, and the Nines desire for calm and comfort. Thus, there is confluence between our inner
world point issues and our desire to project them, or conversely, perhaps to conceal them, as illustrated
by the study’s findings.
The study findings suggest that our desire is to project an image congruent with our inner sense of
self. The most common fear reported by study participants was of having an image that was not being
perceived as intended. Issues with image appear to be subject to the same motivation as our respective
Enneagram type.
The world of image may appear to be very different from the world of spirituality and the Enneagram.
Yet, if one looks more closely, one will find that the journey of self-discovery is layered in clothing and
protected by many aspects of the human condition - one’s personality, Enneagram type, and personal
image. Sharing this information with others has proven to be akin to discovering a jewel, shaping
and illuminating that which is already brilliant, rather than painting on a veneer limited by external
standards alone. The image that is harmonious with one’s internal image is powerful, as it honors one’s
true nature -- a foundation upon which a deeper representation of the inner self can be externalized and
fully expressed.

Enneastyle Lexicon Language Usage
Enneastyle Questionnaire Example
Overall, the Enneagram Types used similar words to describe themselves on the Enneastyle Questionnaire.
Any of the 9 Types can potentially use some of the same words. However, it is the ‘cluster’ or group of
words used that suggests one’s potential Type, Tritype and/or Instinctual Type.
This is an example of a 17 year old, female Type Four that was new to the Enneagram at the time she took
this test. We can readily see keywords we assign to Type Four along with words used by Type Six, Type
Five and the Sexual Instinctual Type. This suggested that this test taker was most likely a sexual Four.
After working with this test taker, we confirmed she is a Sexual 469.
The Fours primarily choose the words: deep, tasteful, beauty, intellectual, emotional, aesthetic,
accomplished, vulgar, crass, intuitive, empathetic, introspective, soulful, and sensitive. In this example we
can see that our test taker chose the word ‘deep’ as her first adjective. She also chose the words ‘soulful and
emotional’. These are words the Type Four often chooses, but they are also the words used by the Sexual
Instinctual Types. More specifically, when this test taker answered the question of greatest weakness we
can clearly see her answer as the Type Four’s focus of attention. She stated:
8. What is your greatest weakness: Too emotional.
9. Why: I love to dwell on my emotions, esp. negative ones, to use them as inspiration for my artwork.
This is a hallmark of Four. The Type Four seeks meaning in their suffering and they often dwell on their
emotional states to create art. This helps them to process painful events. Another hallmark of Type 4 is the
word “royalty-like.” In thousands of questionnaires, only the Four used words indicating royalty.
We can also see words that Fives use such as private and thoughtful and words Sixes use such as thoughtful,
sensitive, and emotional. Sixes most commonly uses kittens and puppies as their creature.
In addition, we can see words that the Sexual Instinctual Type uses such as deep, soulful, elegant and the
phrase “An expression of who I am.” Also we can see that the test taker, like most Fours, has a core fear
of being ordinary. Our test taker stated in the question ‘ I avoid:’ “Appearing to be just another ordinary
person.” She also used the word ‘sensitive,’ which is most common with the Fours and then the Sixes. She
also referenced a fear of being shallow, which is also a key focus for the Fours.
Four is also the only Type that made a distinction of having a focus on their outside image versus the
internal image, often saying they wanted them to match. She stated in the question 19. “I am: Friendly on
the outside, but once you get to know more, you’ll realize it’s so hard to get to know me.”

One
Moral Perfectionist
Overview
Ones want to be accurate, thorough, fair and objective. More importantly, Ones want to be respectable, to do what
is right and what they feel is appropriate. Ones see themselves as rational, orderly and principled. Ones would like
others to see them as reliable, responsible and ethical. Ones idealized image is that they are hardworking and do
what is right.
Gifted with a strong sense of purpose, Ones have high ideals and like to follow protocol, policies and procedures.
They are sincere, earnest and diligent. Ones like to excel and take pride in doing things well. Ones value honesty,
integrity and objectivity. Conscientious and methodical, Ones focus on paying close attention to detail. Earnest and
hardworking, Ones are particular and constantly strive to improve. Ones take action from their hearts because, for
Ones, the heart is the only thing that is truly perfect.
Ones have strong internal critics that remind them of what they should and shouldn’t do. At times, they can be
judgmental and critical of others, telling them what they think is right. Ones behave this way because they want
to help people avoid mistakes. Privately, they criticize themselves for their shortcomings far more than they ever
criticize anyone else. Ones believe that there is only one way to do things and that is the right way. As a perfectionist,
they feel that if something is worth doing, it is worth doing right. As a result, they are only satisfied with what
appeals to their strong sense of what is moral. Idealistic, Ones naturally want to educate others to help them improve
themselves. Life for Ones is about adhering to strong internal standards. Sometimes, they wish they could let go
and have more fun. This can be hard for Ones to do, unless they are on vacation, because of their strong work ethic.
Ones innately recognize that in most situations there are standard operational procedures that must be followed to
be responsible and to maintain a sense of order. Because Ones are so hard on themselves, they long for positive
feedback, serenity and happiness.
Need
Ones need to know what is expected of them so they can act accordingly and excel. Ones want to know what is
considered appropriate and to do things by the book to avoid making a mistake. They continuously strive for selfimprovement and expect others to do the same. Ones always act in accordance with their high standards, moral
beliefs, philosophies and principles, instead of basing their behaviors on another person’s rules.
Avoid
Ones avoid impropriety, irresponsibility, high risks and/or extremes. Most importantly, Ones avoid expressing
anger, feeling that to do so is wrong or inappropriate. Underneath that is their attitude of moral superiority, the need
for correctness, and a deep fear of being wrong, bad, evil or corruptible. They prefer what is practical, grounded and
stable, and avoid appearing silly or frivolous to others.

Virtue
One’s integrity, wisdom and adherence to sound procedure can bring clarity and guidance to a confused world. Ones
are true pioneers with the ability to envision utopia, and the discipline to put in the hard work necessary to make it
happen. Like a White Knight, Ones are not afraid to act according to their strong convictions— even if their actions
go against the beliefs of their parents, boss or society. Ones have a special gift for teaching and enjoy helping people
learn and improve.
Vice
One’s vice is resentment because they work hard to repress negative emotions. They feel that showing overt anger
demonstrates a loss of control, so they suppress their wrath when others are not working as hard as them and don’t
feel the same level of responsibility that they do. At times, Ones can be self-righteous, overly strict and rigid.
They can become irritable, nit picking and critical when others don’t abide by their lofty moral code and need for
procedures. Ones should remember that people think of them as teachers, and the best teachers lead by example,
not criticism.
Attention
Ones attention goes outward, to the environment, to creating improvements, correcting imperfections and righting
what is wrong. In their search for what is perfect, they can become mired in details and lose sight of their original
goal and intention. Often it is more important to complete a task than to wait for perfection.
Spiritual Path
Their spiritual journey is to reclaim a sense of serenity. Even flaws may have a purpose. Ones should know that true
perfection and spiritual growth will come when they realize that all things are inherently perfect just as they are.
Mantra
True perfection already exists in every moment, and there is nothing that needs to be reformed or improved in the
eyes of God.
Wing
The Enneagram Type 1 with the 9 Wing desires to appear aristocratic. They see themselves as classic, casually
elegant, simple, relaxed and natural.
The Enneagram Type 1 with the 2 Wing, desires to appear cultivated. They see themselves as put together,
fashionable, moody, apropos and unique.

I am Appropriate
Dress to Repress
Most Ones saw some aspect of their image style as wholesome, appropriate, put-together, and classic.
The basic desire of the Ones was to be “right,” and they needed standards, beliefs, and/or philosophies to
know what is appropriate. Ones seemed to need some form of acceptance, class, or the “right” clothing
and accessories to support their image. The basic fear of most Ones was of being “criticized,” and so they
avoided impropriety and repressed anything that did not support their chosen standards.
Ones wished to appear updated but traditional, classic with drama, stunning yet accessible, solid but
whimsical, stable but fun, appropriate but ever-changing, radiant but exemplary, and simple yet interesting.
It is noteworthy that many of the Ones described themselves as moody, irritable or contrary as part of
their image. When interviewed, the Ones explained that the irritability/moodiness was really only two
moods. They either felt right, happy and content when things were going well, or irritable and sarcastic
when things were going wrong. That explains their ever-changing moods. In addition, many of the Ones
had never considered what their personal symbols might be, and those who did chose hearts, cherubs and
angels. The Ones explained that it was the “perfection” of the heart that was so appealing, and that there
had always been some underlying desire to be angelic.

Two
Nurturing Advisor
Overview
Twos want to be appealing, giving, caring and heartfelt. More importantly, they want to be needed, considered
important and appreciated for their efforts. Twos see themselves as approachable, nurturing and thoughtful. Twos
would like others to them as empathetic, supportive and altruistic. The Twos idealized image is that they are a loving
and helpful person.
Twos have a warm, sociable and enthusiastic personality. Twos want to be liked, and are always ready with an easy
smile, helpful advice, or a friendly compliment. Relationship orientated, they reach out to others and are often the
first one to lend a hand. Good listeners, Twos focus on the needs and concerns of others. Twos usually know what
people need and feel, and enjoy taking on the kind of role where they can show their giving and generous nature.
Twos have developed the gift of flattery to the level of an art form because they see how this skill can be a currency
for creating intimate rapport.
Secretly, Twos can feel needy and vulnerable, feeling that they must earn the right to be loved. Avoiding a deep
sense of loneliness, Twos find ways to connect to and be of service to others. Twos have an innate sense of what to
say or do to make people feel seen and admired. Like a fairy godmother or godfather, they like to do special things
for people and surprise them with unexpected gifts. Twos take note of others’ desires and try to fulfill them. Twos
take pride in their ability to comfort and support others.
Twos find it gratifying to be of service, and are drawn to people that have power and influence. Twos enjoy being
the power behind the throne and make themselves appealing by being indispensable. Always ready to be helpful,
others often depend on them. Twos have innate people skills and value interpersonal relationships above all else.
Their extraordinary insight about what people need, and their ability to create mutual trust and affinity, are rare gifts.
They love to be instrumental in helping others achieve their full potential.
Need
Twos need approval, recognition and admiration. Most importantly, they want to be seen as a ‘special’ friend. Twos
go out of their way to notice what is needed and feel motivated when others acknowledge their efforts and express
their appreciation. Sometimes, Twos are so focused on others, that they don’t pay attention to their own unmet needs
and feelings. Twos may feel that in order to have their needs met by others, they must meet other’s needs first.
Avoid
Twos avoid appearing needy or useless. Secretly, Twos fear being ignored and don’t want to feel left out, or be seen
as inconsequential. To be liked, they avoid saying or doing anything that is off-putting or unflattering. Twos feel
much more comfortable giving than receiving. To let themselves receive, they would have to express their own
desires, which feels very vulnerable to the Two. Focused on the needs of others, they may repress their own needs
and feel taken for granted.

Virtue
Demonstrative and friendly, Twos are exceptionally gifted at creating and maintaining relationships. Emotionally
astute, Twos have a way of creating rapport with even the most difficult people. They are softhearted and sympathetic
with others and always seem to notice when someone feels sad or is in distress. Twos intuitively know what is
needed relationally in any given situation, and always seem to know just the right thing to say and do. Twos ability
to sense and satisfy other’s emotional needs is second to none. Twos also have the ability to match others and work
well in tandem with them.
Vice
The vice of the Two is pride, believing that they are the one who sees what people need and can give them what
they want. Though they might not realize it, they may give to people with strings attached. When Twos offer
unsolicited advice or give compulsively, others may perceive them as bossy, overly demonstrative or manipulative.
In an effort to have their needs met, they may promise more than they can deliver. Unconsciously, Twos may feel
needy and want to be taken care of by others. They can become emotional, sullen or overly dramatic, suffering from
psychosomatic illnesses.
Attention
Twos’ attention goes outward toward being helpful, flattering others, meeting people’s needs and finding someone
who can meet their needs. They may be unaware of how much time and energy they spend giving until they feel sad
that others are not able to do the same for them.
Spiritual Path

Three
Successful Achiever
Overview
Threes want to be competent, productive, accomplished and dynamic. More importantly, Threes want to be good
at what they do and to look good doing it. Threes see themselves as ambitious, competitive and industrious. Threes
would like others to see them as a winner with all the signifiers of success. Threes’ idealized image of themselves is
that they are triumphant and victorious.
Threes are goal-oriented. They live by the motto “Why do when you can overdo!” To say that they are self-confident,
ambitious and motivated may be an understatement. Threes want to be seen as the very example of the person who
has it all together and is on their way to the top. Threes take great pleasure in crossing tasks off their ‘to do’ lists
after quickly finishing them. Threes may even write down a task on their list that is already completed just so they
can enjoy the sense of accomplishment they feel when it’s crossed off.
Concerned about having prestige and status, Threes quickly adapt to whatever role is expected of them. Seeking
success through appearances, they are likely to place continuous pressure on themselves to maintain an enviable
image. Threes do this by controlling their emotions, being efficient and maintaining a focused, single-minded
attitude. A natural born performer, Threes try to do their best and to be an ‘all star’ at what ever they attempt.
Continuously seeking attention and admiration, like a strutting peacock, they aren’t afraid to showcase or promote
their talents and ambitions. As there is no second best for them, they keep trying until they reach their goals. They
strive for, and often develop, finesse. Their high level of self-confidence, enthusiasm and energy tends to rub off
on everyone around them. Their positive attitude is often motivating to others. Always on the go and energized by
starting new projects, they can achieve a great deal, but are also at risk of burning out. As soon as Threes reach one
goal, they are on to the next.
Need
Threes need to be the best at whatever they do. They love the sound of applause and the accolades that are given
for a job well done. Threes also need others to praise and acknowledge their achievements. Looking successful in
someone else’s eyes is what motivates them to push themselves harder and accomplish more. Always one to seek
attention through their personal achievements, it is difficult for Threes to relax, stop being the peacock and become
part of the herd.
Avoid
Threes avoid failure, nothingness, being second best or appearing like they cannot accomplish what is expected
of them. The Three may repress or swallow their emotions, seeing them as speed bumps that slow them down on
the way to reaching their goals. This can cause their emotions to come up and surprise them at unexpected times.
Because they have a tendency to mistake what they do for who they are, they can become

Four
Romantic Individualist
Overview
Fours want to be gifted, intuitive, original and unique. More importantly, Fours want to be passionate, true to their
feelings and uniquely authentic. Fours see themselves as sensitive, expressive and spiritual. They would like others
to see them as idealistic, emotionally deep and compassionate. Fours’ idealized image is that they are accomplished
and special.
Motivated by the need to understand and to be understood, Fours desire experiences that are rich with feeling and
meaning. They may find it easier to deal with painful emotions than to deal with the tedium of daily routine. Fours
have the temperament of an artist and long to freely express themselves. Fours feel their emotions deeply and are not
afraid to go emotionally where others fear to tread. This includes having an exquisite, intuitive ability to distinguish
between subtle emotions that others often miss. Painfully self-conscious, Fours are often overly focused on how
different they are from others. A true humanitarian, they have a natural passion for protest. At times intense and
contrarian, Fours are not afraid to think for themselves and voice their point of view.
Nostalgic by nature, Fours often focus on past experiences. This can lead them to deeper insights or to downward
spirals of melancholy and/or painful unresolved feelings. Craving ideal circumstances or love, they often ruminate
on what is missing and perceived to be important. Their tendency towards self-absorption is both an asset and
liability. It can lead the Four to deep personal insights that can benefit everyone while feeding their self-deprecating
sense of humor; it can also make them appear to be self-centered and disinterested in others. Feeling their own inner
world so powerfully, it is good for them to remember that others’ experiences are just as real for them as the Fours’
emotions and experiences are for them.
When Fours step out of the river of their emotions, they can bring forth their many talents into the world and express
them in a way that is extraordinary and original. They are like the lotus flower growing in the mud that is able to
transform emotionally painful experiences into fertilizer for personal growth. Attuned to feelings, they have an
uncommon sensitivity when it comes to dealing with suffering. They are not afraid to hear about someone else’s
troubles, and can be great friends to anyone in emotional pain.
Need
Fours need be seen as artistic, gifted and accomplished. They focus on their individuality and on carving their own
distinct image. Fours need to express their deep feelings and want others to validate their emotions. Whether they
are organizing a living space to reflect their refined tastes, or engaging in an artistic pursuit, it is essential for their
sense of well-being that they express their creativity.
Avoid
Fours avoid feeling lost, disorientated and without personal significance, meaning or direction. They also avoid
appearing inadequate, defective or flawed. Most importantly, Fours have a hidden fear of being emotionally cut off
and abandoned. Fours avoid affectation and anything dull, ordinary, ugly, vulgar, inauthentic or distasteful. .

Five
Investigative Thinker
Overview
Fives want to be intelligent, informed, knowledgeable and perceptive. More importantly, they want to be selfsufficient and not have the entanglements of obligation. They see themselves as intellectual, dispassionate and
investigative. They would like others to see them as rational, logical and scholarly. Their idealized image is that
they are thoughtful and wise.
Private and solitary by nature, Fives like to be invisible until they are ready to reveal themselves. They tend to stay
on the sidelines, preferring to meet the world with their minds. Fives prefer to play the role of detached observer or
investigator. In general, Fives see the world as intrusive, overwhelming and chaotic–often demanding too much and
giving too little in return. As a result, to manage the fear of not knowing, Fives conserve their energy and focus their
attention on acquiring the information necessary to make sense out of the chaos.
Studious and scholarly, Fives develop expertise in any area that is of interest to them, often in more than one field.
Fives believe that knowledge is power and feel it is imperative that they be intellectually astute as a means of
survival. Often scientific, Fives have unparalleled powers of mental perception due in part to their ability to remain
detached and unaffected by their emotions. They have an inquisitive and observant nature with an insatiable appetite
for information. Fives think things through before offering their perceptive insights regarding systems, people, or
how the world works.
Avoiding the glaring light of scrutiny, Fives seek the safety of camouflage. When they feel that they lack intellect,
Fives become withdrawn, isolated and reclusive. Fives see themselves as intense, unexpected, original and different
from others. They are private and introspective, although others may think they are anti-social, secretive, remote,
and eccentric. Fives are not afraid to point out the ‘emperor who has no clothes’ and their wonderful sense of humor
is based on postulating the absurd. Rather than the wave crashing on the shore, Fives have the strength of the
undertow and know exactly when to give or withhold their involvement and information to have the greatest impact.
Need
Fearing intrusion, Fives need to keep themselves hidden and camouflaged. However intellectual they may be, Fives
often feel vulnerable and exposed like an animal without fur. Fives need time alone to recharge and for others to not
place high demands on their time or energy. It is essential for their well-being that their minds are clear, their lives
uncluttered, and that they have the autonomy to control their time.
Avoid
Fives avoid standing out and/or being misinterpreted. Fives also avoid pretentiousness or ostentatious displays. Fives
may find small talk or a brainstorming session at work to be an insufferable waste of time. Fives fear annihilation,
contamination, and being fully embodied – as they feel more comfortable being with their thoughts than in their
body. Because Fives prefer to live a life that isn’t weighed down by attachments – either material or relational – they
also avoid surplus of any kind. The one exception might be a library of resources.

Loyal Guardian
Overview
Sixes want to be safe and secure, to fit in and belong. More importantly, they want to have certainty and security,
putting their faith in a trusted authority, belief system or tradition. Sixes see themselves as loyal, friendly, conservative
and/or cautious. Sixes would like others to see them as trustworthy, dedicated and reliable. Their idealized image is
that they are supportive, responsible and dutiful.
Sixes are innately curious with a wonderful sense of humor. A bundle of contradictions, Sixes can be wary and
cautious one minute and/or rebellious and courageous the next. Sixes possess an endearing childlike reactivity
and often take the position of the devil’s advocate to promote a sense of fair play. Depending on the situation,
Sixes can be friendly and outgoing or reserved and skeptical. A ‘good soldier’, they prefer the role of buddy, loyal
family member or trusted employee. They can be a reluctant authority, because they fear that taking on a leadership
position might make them a target for opposition. As a result, Sixes need to chose their positions carefully. To
manage anxiety, they seek comic relief.
Sixes are attracted to people who are strong, protective and/or have prestige. Sixes seek trusted, reliable authorities
and allies. They often identify with the underdog and are distrustful of people’s hidden agendas, which makes
them slow to trust new acquaintances. Keenly aware that they could be duped or taken advantage of, they pay
attention to insincerity and inconsistencies. To determine if someone is trustworthy, Sixes watch for and question
any discrepancy they observe. Once someone passes the testing process, Sixes become deeply committed and a
most loyal friend. They are devoted to their friends and family, and show allegiance over individuality.
Seeking certainty, Sixes tend to be cynical. Because Sixes are afraid of being unprepared or caught off guard, they
often rehearse in their mind what they might say or do. Imaginative and visual, they can often see what might go
wrong. They may even enjoy scaring themselves by thinking of worst-case scenarios so that they will never feel
paralyzed by the fear of not knowing what to say or do. Although Sixes are afraid to be afraid, they can learn mind
over matter and are capable of being profoundly dutiful and heroic. Once Sixes let go of doubts, their fear turns into
excitement and they can surprise themselves with unexpected acts of valor.
Need
Sixes need predictability, a protective authority and the security of feeling that they belong. They long for trustworthy
guidance to manage the feelings of fear and doubt. They can be a “proof junkie” who needs to test people, ideas and
beliefs over and over again to see if they are worthy of their loyalty. They tend to need reassurance, encouragement
and positive feedback from friends and loved ones.
Avoid
Sixes avoid deviance, uncertainty and anything that is different from any group or idea to which they ascribe. They
are highly motivated to avoid disappointing friends or authority figures. Fear and doubt can undermine their sense

of safety. Sixes are afraid of fear, submission and cowardice, so they vacillate between loyalty and rebelliousness

Like the hummingbird that moves in a frenzied blur from flower to flower, they seek the sweet nectar of excitement,
new experiences, people and travel. If life gets them down, they escape their anxiety and boredom through variety
and activity. Uneasiness is kept at bay by keeping busy with interesting and diverse experiences. Seeking a positive

and monotony out of routine tasks and turning the mundane into the magical.

change their mind at the last minute.
Avoid
Sevens avoid boredom, sadness and emotional pain. Sevens fear limitation, feeling trapped or appearing inferior.
Feeling incomplete, confined, or missing out are among their deepest fears. They avoid painful emotions because
they are afraid that they will become overwhelmed if they talk about them or feel them. Sevens also avoid negative
people as they can bring them down. This can lead them to avoid the present by living in a future fantasy of plans.

Eight
Protective Challenger
Overview
Eights want to be open, honest, direct and straightforward. More importantly, they want to be independent, make
their own decisions and direct their own course. They see themselves as assertive, decisive and resourceful. Eights
would like others to see them as strong, fair-minded and magnanimous. Their idealized image is that they are
protective and powerful.
Eights are charismatic and exude confidence. Eights have a take-charge, no-nonsense, can-do attitude. They have
great pride in their ability to face, endure and overcome adversity, believing that what doesn’t kill them makes them
stronger. Their assertive stance and imposing presence makes them a ‘force of nature’. They like challenges and
usually overcome the kinds of obstacles that limit or deter others. Eights have a lust for life and want to experience
everything to the fullest. They believe that ‘too much of a good thing is almost enough’.
An Eight’s word is their bond and they do not suffer fools gladly. People are occasionally intimidated by Eights and
have a tendency to misinterpret their direct manner as insensitivity. Self-possessed, they easily speak their minds
and can come across as too aggressive. Eights believe that being true to themselves means that they say what they
mean and mean what they say. Eights stand up for their beliefs and perceive backing down as a sign of weakness –
even though at times doing so may be common sense and in their own best interest.
Behind the Eight’s tough exterior they are gentle, playful, childlike and innocent. Always one to laugh, they can
see the humor even in the most painful and difficult of circumstances. Eights are also unusually devoted to and
protective of those in their ‘inner circle,’ often secretly going without something themselves so that others will have
what they need. Living by their own personal code of honor, they have a strong sense of justice which compels
them to act whenever they see any kind of injustice taking place. This often inspires great loyalty from those who
see them as a protective authority.
Need
Eights need to be their own authority, and seek independence to be a master and commander of their own lives. They
have the need to feel confident, self-reliant and in charge of their emotions. Eights don’t need to be liked, but they
do have a strong need to be respected. Eights also need a sense of fairness and justice in their dealings with others,
or they are likely to assert their willful and stubborn nature.
Avoid
Eights avoid being weak, vulnerable, controlled or manipulated. Secretly, they fear being caught off guard, harmed
and/or humiliated. Being mistreated and at the mercy of injustice are among their deepest fears. Eights resist and
defy anything that is imposed upon them. Fearing deprivation, they avoid ever having to go without what they deem
essential to their survival. Eights do everything in their power to avoid being dependent on anyone or anything.
They have a distaste for mediocrity and anything that feels contrived.

Virtue
Eights’ greatest strength is their sense of justice and desire to protect the weak, vulnerable, down-trodden and
under-represented. Big hearted and generous by nature, they willingly protect others even at their own expense. A
person of their word, they stand up for what they believe in and deliver what they have promised. They have the
ability to quickly assess a situation, cut to the chase and ‘call a spade a spade’, simplifying what initially appears to
be complex and confusing. This ability to instinctively see the truth in any given situation and act decisively makes
them natural born leaders.
Vice
The Eight’s vice is excess and going to extremes. Whatever they like, they want more of, and whatever they do,
they overdo at full speed. Quick to respond, Eights can over react and come on too strong. They can be too much,
too intense, and unwilling to self-limit. This can lead to escalating conflict due to an over reliance on their own truth
and self-defined justice. Under stress they can become myopic, believing their truth as ‘The Truth’. Seeing life as
a battlefield or a contest of wills, they can become confrontational and have difficulty backing down or admitting
defeat. Eights have a tendency to push things to the edge and sometimes may run over others in the process.
Attention
The Eight’s attention goes to issues of power— who has it and most importantly, if it is being used fairly. They
instinctively sense a power vacuum and they often step in to fill it. They are not interested in subtle nuance but
rather in the big picture, much like a general surveying a battlefield. Eights can be relentless in their search for truth,
justice, power and influence.
Spiritual Path
The spiritual journey of the Eight to reclaim their sense of innocence. Spiritual growth will come when they can
acknowledge a higher truth and a higher power. As the king or queen of the jungle, they can either take advantage
of others with their powerful and commanding presence or make the jungle a safe place for everyone.
Mantra
True strength comes from surrendering the need to have power and control. Eights should remember that sensitivity
to others and acknowledging their own tender emotions is a sign of strength, not a sign of weakness. Cynicism and
insensitive teasing are red flags that they are feeling hurt and disempowered.
Wing
The Enneagram Type 8 with the 7 Wing, desires to appear commanding. They see themselves as outspoken,
innovative, intense, daring, striking, attractive and tenderhearted.
The Enneagram Type 8 with the 9 Wing, desires to appear solid. They see themselves as earthy, simple, honest,
practical, authoritative, solid, wise and considerate.

I am Authentic
Dress to Express
Most Eights saw some aspect of their image style as authentic, simple, dignified and expressive. The basic
desire of Eights was to be “impressive.” Eights seemed to need some form of independence, comfort,
movement and plenty to support their image. The basic fear of most Eights was “to conform,” and so they
avoided anything mediocre or contrived and dressed to express their authenticity.
Eights wished to appear charismatic, dignified, authoritative, direct, independent, to have panache,
contradictory, powerful, unexpected, elegant, comfortably creative, colorful, liontine, contemporary,
practical, commanding and indifferent.
It was noteworthy that most of the Eights seemed to agree that they dressed for themselves and “didn’t
care” if they were accepted or in fashion. However, when interviewed, the truth was Eights did care, but
the real desire was simply to fully express themselves, and image was an area of vulnerability for them.
Eights acknowledged that they never avoided conflict, but also felt they did not seek it out; and so in some
manner, they did wish to be acceptable. Ironically, Eights felt the most constricted in being able to express
themselves, because historically they have at times done it so poorly, and the reception has not been what
was intended. The theme “dress to express” could have applied to many of the points, but perhaps best
represents the Eights desire to freely express themselves and still be accepted. Over all, Eights were the
most comfortable with their own image, tending to be very clear about their preferences from a very early
age.

Nine
Peaceful Mediator
Overview
Nines want to be agreeable, peaceful, natural and comfortable. More importantly, they want to be calm, and if at
all possible, to avoid conflict. Nines see themselves as accepting, unassuming and laid-back. Nines would like
others to see them as humble, easygoing and approachable. Their idealized self-image is that they are content and
harmonious.
Desiring union, harmony and unconditional love, Nines get along with most people that they meet. Considerate and
gentle, they have an innate ability to make people feel comfortable and at ease. Nines are very accepting and readily
empathize with others. Focused on the well-being of others, they may at times lose sight of their own agenda. More
intellectual than they let on, they have a natural gift for seeing many different points of view. Nines are able to see all
the shades of gray in any given situation and have a way of diffusing conflict and being calm and steady in a crisis.
Focusing on similarities rather than differences, they make an excellent mediator and peacemaker.
Nines don’t want to be drawn into conflict and avoid being pressured to react. Self-forgetting by nature, they repress
their anger and neglect their needs in order to avoid the discomfort that comes with conflict. Because Nines may lose
themselves in life’s simple pleasures, the needs of others, or the basic task of living, they may never go after what
they truly want. Nines may struggle to find their one true passion. At times, they may be complacent and minimize
anything that is upsetting.
Often the strong, silent type, Nines have the power of patience, persistence and resistance. Nines see themselves as
having stamina and take pride in their ability to endure. They tend to procrastinate and take longer than others to
make up their mind. When they do make a decision, they are like an unstoppable elephant pursuing its goals. Not
one to self-promote, they would rather be discovered than announce themselves. Others may underestimate them
due to their casual and nonchalant attitude. This is nearly always a mistake—one that they are happy to let others
make because they prefer not to flaunt their talents or appear egotistical.
Need
Nines need simplicity, peace, harmony and to be appreciated. Because they are highly empathetic, they need to
know that the people around them are relaxed in order for them to be able to relax. To truly be at ease, Nines need
to have their creature comforts and are unwilling go without them. Publicly, they may go along to get along, but
when in private they make sure that they have what makes them feel happy and comfortable.
Avoid
Nines avoid conflict, complications and judgmental people. Being shut down, overlooked and not included by
others are among their greatest fears. Nines are afraid of being loveless and of not receiving or being able to give
love. Paradoxically, their avoidance of anger and conflict can lead to more conflict. Nines don’t want to deal
with anything until they are good and ready. It is hard for them to say no, so yes means maybe and maybe means
absolutely not!

Virtue
Nines’ virtues are their acceptance, peacefulness and ability to see universal truths. Open and receptive, they easily
relate to others. When Nines are at their best, they can be both personally and universally connected. Whether they
prefer simple pleasures or intellectual pursuits, they are always seeking what is harmonious. Nines often show their
love by working hard, and they can make great contributions to others. Down to earth, steady, patient and easygoing,
they bring a sense of calm to any given situation. Steadfast and persistent, Nines are able to endure even the most
tedious and difficult circumstances.
Vice
Nines’ vices are indolence, inaction or sloth. This can lead to self-forgetting and neglecting their own needs and/
or the needs of others. Their inattention and forgetfulness can manifest as “crimes of omission”. They may also be
stubborn or turn a blind eye to someone in need. Everyone thinks that Nines are on their side and can misinterpret
their understanding as agreement. Overly avoiding conflict, they are most likely to hurt the people around them
by not keeping promises due to their lack of follow-through. This can make for a passive-aggressive stance in life
where Nines don’t see the impact of their inaction and can appear callous or indifferent.
Attention
Nines’ attention goes to identifying with and merging with others. Their soft, pillowy energy tends to spread
outwards and become diffuse. Because they sense and champion the needs and feelings of whatever group they are
in, they are a natural group anchors. To avoid feeling tension and conflict, they may reach for substitutes for love by
eating, focusing on the minutia or getting lost in unimportant tasks.
Spiritual Path
The Nines’ spiritual journey is to reclaim their sense of right action and awaken from the coma of self-forgetting.
Spiritual growth will come to them as they reclaim, define and assert their sense of self in the world.
Mantra
True love is action and not passive acceptance. To be truly loving requires that they define their values and take
a stand for what they believe is important– regardless of the conflict it may cause. Indecisiveness and a fear of
upsetting people can prevent them from taking appropriate action. Remember that no decision is a decision, and a
“stitch in time saves nine”.

I am Unassuming
Dress to Dress
Most Nines saw some aspect of their image style as nonchalant, casual, leisurely, unstructured and
natural. The basic desire of Nines was to be “comfortable.” Nines seemed to need some form of harmony,
motivation, appreciation and simplicity to support their image. The basic fear of most Nines was of being
“overlooked,” and yet they avoided complication and dressed to be unassuming and at ease, to receive
attention and to assure harmony with others.
Nines wished to appear calm, casually elegant, haphazardly sophisticated, coordinated but not too obvious,
confident but friendly, casual yet professional, tailored but soft, artful but approachable, simple with ease,
and nice.
It was noteworthy that most of the Nines shared the common element of comfort, as it appeared in every
category. They also chose words that expressed their internal rather than external image, and chose
universal symbols such as the sun, moon, stars and world. When interviewed, the Nines indicated that
the symbols represented a desire to make a difference in the world, and to be united with the universe.
By being unassuming, they hoped that their external presentation communicated their internal desire
for peace, harmony and, most importantly, freedom from conflict. Nines also reported that they enjoyed
clothing that expressed their sense of humor.
